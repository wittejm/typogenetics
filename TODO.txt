⏺ OK — what if bases are conserved?                                                                                               
                                                                                                                                  
  Right now insertion is free. inc conjures a C from nowhere. What if there's a free base pool — a finite supply of A, C, G, T    
  floating in the soup?
                                                                                                                                  
  - Insertion (ina/inc/ing/int) draws from the free pool. If that base isn't available, the insertion fails — the amino is a no-op
   and the enzyme continues.
  - Deletion (del) returns the deleted base to the free pool.
  - Eviction returns all of a strand's bases to the free pool.
  - Total base count across all strands + free pool is conserved.

  Here's why this might break the C/G trap naturally, without any designed selection pressure:

  In the current system, inc and ing spam C's and G's from nothing. They win because they can grow forever for free. With
  conservation, they run out of fuel. The free C and G supply gets depleted as the inserters consume it.

  Now: what frees up bases? Cutting and deletion. Cut comes from AC duplet. Del comes from AG duplet. Both require A-containing
  strands — exactly the strands that the C/G trap kills off.

  So conservation creates a natural mutualism:
  - C/G strands are good inserters (consumers of bases)
  - A-containing strands are good cutters/deleters (producers of free bases)
  - Neither can survive without the other
  - The C/G monoculture starves itself

  That's not a thumb on the scale. That's a conservation law producing ecological interdependence — the same way real catabolism
  (breaking down) feeds anabolism (building up).

  You could start the soup with, say, 500 total bases distributed across initial strands plus a small free pool, and watch whether
   the economy self-organizes.
