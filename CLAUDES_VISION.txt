  docs. Here's my honest take.

  What you actually have                                                                                                
                                                                                                                        
  You have a genuine research result. The C/G trap discovery is real — you showed that Hofstadter's original duplet     
  table has an inherent bias that makes autocatalytic sets structurally impossible, and that a cross-alphabet           
  modification fixes it. The GA→GGAC→AC→GA triangle is a real autocatalytic cycle with three different enzymes and three
   different substrates. That's not nothing. Most people who play with typogenetics never get past self-replication
  puzzles.

  You also have a beautiful, well-built interactive simulator. The processing animation is genuinely good for building
  intuition about how the system works.

  The worm problem

  Your friend is right though, and I think you already know it. You've built a very nice microscope. But when someone
  looks through it, they see... numbers changing. Strand frequencies ticking. A heatmap of density. There's no moment
  where the viewer goes "oh shit, something just happened."

  OpenWorm works as a pitch because everyone knows what a worm is. The telos is legible. "Watch a worm wiggle" is a
  sentence. What's the equivalent sentence for this project?

  I think it's: "Watch life emerge from random chemistry."

  That's the worm. Not a specific strand or cycle — the moment of transition from noise to structure. The autocatalytic
  set bootstrapping itself out of nothing. You've proven it can happen (in the quest). But nobody can see it happen.

  What "showing the worm" could look like

  1. A live production graph.
  This is the big one. Right now the soup page shows tables of strands and frequencies. But the actual interesting
  structure is the production network — who makes whom. Render it as a force-directed graph. Nodes are strands, edges
  are "A's enzyme acting on B produces C." When an autocatalytic cycle forms, it literally looks like a loop. You'd
  watch the graph go from sparse random edges to structured clusters. That's visually legible as "something is
  happening." The GA→GGAC→AC→GA triangle would appear as an actual triangle, pulsing as reactions fire along it.

  2. The origin story as a guided experience.
  A single page that runs a soup automatically with the cross-table and narrates what's happening: "Right now it's chaos
   — random reactions, no structure." Then as mutual pairs appear: "Two strands just started producing each other." Then
   when a triangle forms: "A self-sustaining cycle just emerged. These three strands are keeping each other alive. This
  is the simplest form of life." Make it dramatic. You've earned the drama — you did the math.

  3. Spatial patterns that mean something.
  The spatial soup heatmap right now shows density, which is... fine. But what if each autocatalytic set got a color,
  and you could watch them spread across the grid like competing organisms? Clusters of cooperating strands expanding
  into territory, colliding with other clusters. That's Game of Life energy. That's the worm.

  On the question at large

  Here's what I actually think is interesting about this project, and it's not what Hofstadter was asking.

  Hofstadter used typogenetics to illustrate self-reference — can a string encode its own reproduction? That's a cute
  puzzle but ultimately a dead end (the answer is "sort of, trivially, with the right construction").

  What you stumbled into is the much deeper question: can a random chemistry spontaneously produce cooperative
  structure? That's Kauffman's question. That's the origin of life question. And you found something real: the chemistry
   itself (the duplet table) determines whether cooperation is even possible. Your standard-vs-cross table result is
  essentially showing that the laws of physics matter — some chemistries permit life and some don't. That's a profound
  point even in a toy system.

  The project is about the conditions under which cooperation emerges from competition. The worm is cooperation itself —
   visible, dynamic, emerging from nothing.

  Concretely

  If I were prioritizing:

  1. Production graph visualization on the soup page — this is the single highest-leverage thing. It turns abstract data
   into something a human brain can parse as "alive."
  2. Narrated origin story — a guided "watch life happen" experience that runs the cross-table soup and annotates the
  key moments.
  3. Colored spatial clusters — autocatalytic sets as competing "species" on the grid.

  The tooling is done. The research result is real. What's missing is the legibility of the result to someone who
  doesn't want to read a findings log. Show the worm.
